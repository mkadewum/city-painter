City Painter

The promised land isn't across any ocean

When the celestial construction ended God stepped back to admire His work.

  • A billboard glowed with His final tag: "I have built you a garden city. Now go - paint your own." And somewhere in the urban symphony, you could almost hear heaven's approval: "See? This is very good." He showed us that a city isn’t just steel and glass, but a living thing, breathing with the hopes of those who walk its streets. Imagine a man who could wake up one morning and decide to build a city before the next rainy season. Not with endless meetings and foreign loans, but with his two hands, the sweat of his people, and the dreams buried in their bones.
  • They say truth comes to those who build with love, and peace grows where the poor and rich work side by side. In Ayedade village, where the soil once tasted bitter with broken promises, ordinary hands mixed red earth and moonlight to create something extraordinary. Street by street, they wove poverty into plenty, proving paradise wasn't some distant dream but something growing beneath their feet - a vibrant city rising from African soil, for African souls. This new Eden rose between moon cycles, its streets pulsing with the rhythm of the global village. Solar panels danced with thatched roofs, slums transformed into gardens bursting with cassava and roses, and at its heart, a club where elders and tech kids shared stories under the master of the universe's smiling gaze. The walls hummed ancestral proverbs by day and glowed with constellations by night.
  • When the work was done, only wet cement bore witness to the builder's final truth. The promised land isn't across any ocean. Children now chase rainbows through streets where their parents once saw only despair, no more 'japa' dreams, no more hungry bellies, just children laughing in the streets they once fled. A place where tall buildings wore the patterns of our grandmothers’ wrappers, where the streets knew when to slow down for children playing and the walls whispered wisdom in languages we had almost forgotten. Even the air smelled different—like rain on fresh earth, like the first bite of a ripe mango. And in the center of it all, a fountain that sometimes, on holy nights, flowed with something sweeter than water enough to heal old wounds. Africa needs no saviors when her children remember their power. The world becomes a better place not through wishes, but through hands that plant, minds that innovate, and hearts that refuse to accept poverty as destiny. Heaven is not just a wish, it is a place we create.

Now go - paint your own."

-- Olayanju Micheal Adewumi & Olayanju Emmanuel Damilola (aka)-Two brothers
